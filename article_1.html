Diffusion Models
from First Principles

Is it just a "Drunkard's Walk" to a cat? Understanding generative AI through Langevin sampling, mathematical physics, and a very lost hiker in the desert.

Deep Dive · ~18 min read · Generative Modelling · Mathematics

If you have spent any time looking into modern AI, you have seen the standard explanation for Diffusion Models:

The Forward Process: You take an image and slowly add Gaussian noise until it's just a cloud of static.

The Reverse Process: You teach a neural network to "remove" that noise step-by-step to recover the image.

It sounds simple. Intuitive, even. But if you are like me, the "intuition" didn't quite click at first. It felt a bit like a magic trick — and it left me with a long list of "Whys":

  • Why does adding noise work in the first place? If our goal is to create data, why do we start by destroying it?
  • What exactly is "noise removal"? Is the network really just a super-powered Photoshop denoiser, or is something deeper happening?
  • Is the noise "structured"? We are told the network removes noise to find an image, but is that noise so special that its removal magically reveals a cat?
  • The biggest contradiction: Why do we add even more random Gaussian noise during the reverse process? If we are trying to clean the image, why are we still throwing dirt on it as we go?

In reality, that final step — the one where we "remove noise" while simultaneously adding more — is governed by a beautiful piece of math. In actual Diffusion papers, you'll see a formula that looks like this:

$$x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t + (1 - \alpha_t) \nabla \log p_t(x_t) \right) + \color{#c8620a}{\sigma_t \mathbf{z}_t}$$
The amber term — a fresh burst of random Gaussian noise added back at every single step

Note the highlighted term: That is a fresh burst of random Gaussian noise we add back in at every single step. It seems like we are sabotaging the cleanup, but as we will see, this "stochasticity" is the secret ingredient.

I realized that to understand the "how," I had to stop looking at it as "image cleaning" and start looking at it from First Principles.

In this series, we aren't going to attack the problem structurally or follow a textbook table of contents. Instead, we are going to explore different mathematical and physical concepts and try to map them back to the world of Diffusion. By connecting these dots, we can build a mental model that actually makes sense.

This series will not deal with specific neural network architectures (like U-Nets or Transformers) or the niche implementation choices that different papers might have made. Our goal is purely to understand the fundamental idea of Diffusion and Generative Modelling from the ground up.

In that effort, I am starting our journey with Langevin Sampling.

Section 01What is Generation, Really?

If we strip away all the AI buzzwords, generation is nothing but sampling.

Whenever we talk about "generating" something — be it an image, a sentence, or a protein sequence — what we are actually doing is picking a single point out of a massive collection of possibilities. How we are able to sample from a known distribution is the generation process itself.

The Simple Die (Discrete Sampling)

If I ask you to "generate" a random number between 1 and 6, you reach for a die. When you roll that die and it lands on a 4, you have successfully "generated" data.

When we use a die, we are working with discrete variables. This is what mathematicians call a Probability Mass Function (PMF). Think of it as a row of 6 buckets. Every time you roll, you have a $1/6$ chance of landing in any specific bucket.

$$P(X=1) = \tfrac{1}{6},\quad P(X=2) = \tfrac{1}{6},\quad \dots,\quad P(X=6) = \tfrac{1}{6}$$

The "generation" here is easy because the options are finite and the probability is known.

Figure 2 — Discrete Sampling: Probability Mass Function (PMF)

Moving to the Continuous World

But what if we aren't rolling a die? What if we are measuring something that doesn't fit into neat buckets — like the exact temperature in your room?

In the continuous world, we use a Probability Density Function (PDF), denoted as $p(x)$. Here, we don't ask "What is the probability of the temperature being exactly $22.000000°C$?" (which is technically zero). Instead, we look at the "density" — how likely is it that the temperature is around $22°C$?

The Image Space (The 1,000,000-Sided Die)

Now, let's map this back to images. Imagine a 1000×1000 pixel grayscale image. Because there are 1,000,000 pixels, one single image is a single point in a 1,000,000-dimensional space.

To "generate" a new image of a cat, we are essentially trying to roll a 1,000,000-dimensional die. In this unfathomably large space, almost every "roll" results in noise. But somewhere in that desert, there are tiny "islands" of high probability — specific configurations of those million pixels that represent a "cat."

The Big Statement: Deep Generative Modeling is nothing more than finding a way to sample from that high-dimensional probability distribution.

The catch? In reality, this distribution is unknown. We have a collection of cat photos, but we don't have the mathematical equation for "Cat-ness." If we had the formula, we could just roll the die and be done. But we don't. We are standing in the dark, in a massive desert of noise, trying to find a tiny island without a map.

Figure 3 — The High-Dimensional Desert: Finding "Cat Island"

Section 02The Wonderful Realization: Sculpting with Noise

Here is the "Aha!" moment. What if I told you that we can sample from any complex distribution using only two ingredients?

  • Gaussian Noise: A simple cloud of random static (The Engine).
  • A Magic Compass: Something technical people call the Score Function (The Guide).

This is the essence of Langevin Sampling.

The Gradient: Finding the "North" of Data

Imagine for a second that we actually had the secret recipe — the analytical formula $P(x)$ — for our target distribution. If we have that formula, we can find its Gradient ($\nabla P(x)$).

A gradient is just a vector that points in the direction of the "steepest ascent." Imagine you are standing on a mountain in a thick fog. That gradient doesn't just tell you which way to go; its magnitude tells you how "steep" the hill is, essentially giving you a hint on how big of a step to take to reach the peak.

The "Log" Twist: The Score Function

But in practice, we don't look at the gradient of $P(x)$. We look at the gradient of the Logarithm of $P(x)$:

$$\text{Score Function} = \nabla \log p(x)$$
The compass that always points toward higher-probability regions

Why the "Log"? In high dimensions, the raw probability $P(x)$ of being a cat is so infinitesimally small when you are in the "noise desert" that the gradient $\nabla P(x)$ becomes effectively zero. The "slope" is too flat to feel. By taking the Logarithm, we mathematically "stretch" the landscape, turning flat plains into steep, feelable slopes.

For the Mathematically Inclined: The Proof

Look at the basic derivative of a logarithm using the chain rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx} \log p(x) = \frac{p'(x)}{p(x)}$$

Example: Suppose our data follows a simple Gaussian: $p(x) = e^{-x^2}$.

Raw Gradient p′(x)
$$p'(x) = -2x\,e^{-x^2}$$
At x = 10 → p(10) ≈ 3.7 × 10⁻⁴⁴
Vanishingly small. Our compass is dead.
Score Function ∇ log p(x)
$$\frac{d}{dx}\log p(x) = \frac{-2x\,e^{-x^2}}{e^{-x^2}} = -2x$$
At x = 10 → score = −20
Clear, strong signal!

The division by $p(x)$ "canceled out" the exponential decay that was killing our signal! 🎯

But How Do We Find This Score Function?

The Secret of the Neural Network

You might argue: "You just said the Probability distribution is unknown, but your derivation explicitly uses P(x)!"

If you caught that, you are 100% correct. This is the "million-dollar problem." I showed the math to prove the point, but in the real world, this is exactly what we use the Neural Network for.

When you see a U-Net architecture "predicting noise," it is actually predicting the Gradient of the Log Probability. It is astonishing to realize that after all, it was not "noise" it was predicting — it was the score. These gradients look like noise when we visualize them in high dimensions, but they are actually precise instructions for our walk.

Section 03The Langevin Walk: A Helicopter Drop into Noise

Now we have our ingredients. Let's walk through building the image intuitively.

Imagine you are dropped from a helicopter at a random point $x_0$ in a 1,000,000-dimensional desert. You see nothing but static. But you have your trusty Score Function Compass.

The Step

You check the compass at $x_0$. It tells you how much and in which direction to move:

$$x_1 = x_0 + \underbrace{\eta \,\nabla \log p(x_0)}_{\text{The Step}}$$
η (eta) = Step Size (Learning Rate). How much we trust the compass.

Here, $\eta$ represents how much we trust our compass.

The Optimization Trap

If you only followed the compass, everyone dropped from the helicopter would converge to the exact same "perfect" average cat. We'd lose all variety.

The "Jiggle": Introducing Randomness

To see the whole island, you need to "dance" as you walk. Every time you take a step toward the peak, you take a tiny, random hop in a random direction.

This gives us the Langevin Equation:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \underbrace{\eta \,\nabla \log p(x_t)}_{\text{The Compass Guide (Drift)}} + \underbrace{\color{#c8620a}{\sqrt{2\eta}\;\mathbf{z}_t}}_{\text{The Random Jiggle (Diffusion)}}$$
The amber term is what prevents collapse to a single point — it is the secret to diverse generation

Why a "Gaussian" Jiggle scaled by $\sqrt{2\eta}$?

Why that specific factor? It's about Equilibrium.

  • The $\sqrt{\eta}$ Scaling: In Brownian motion, random kicks grow with the square root of time. This factor ensures the "push" of noise and the "pull" of the drift stay in a consistent ratio as you change step sizes.
  • The Factor of 2: This is the mathematical "sweet spot" that ensures the walk settles into the exact shape of $p(x)$ — not a wider or narrower approximation of it.
  • Why Gaussian? Because nature is Gaussian. The sum of millions of tiny, independent kicks (water molecules hitting a dust particle) always results in a Gaussian distribution — the Central Limit Theorem. It is the only "jiggle" that allows for perfectly smooth exploration.
Figure 5 — The Langevin Walk: Drift + Diffusion Together

Section 04The Grand Connection: Mapping Langevin Back to Diffusion

Look at the "scary" formula from the start side-by-side with our walk:

Actual Diffusion Reverse Step:

$$x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t + (1 - \alpha_t) \nabla \log p_t(x_t) \right) + \color{#c8620a}{\sigma_t \mathbf{z}_t}$$

Our Langevin Walk:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \eta\,\nabla \log p(x_t) + \color{#c8620a}{\sqrt{2\eta}\,\mathbf{z}_t}$$
The math is the same. The coefficients are just "volume knobs" — $\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}$ and $(1-\alpha_t)$ are engineered so the noise schedule anneals correctly over $T$ steps. We have successfully proven that Generation is nothing but sampling.

But the Rabbit Hole Goes Deeper...

Even though we've demystified the "walk," many questions remain:

Why does it settle? What invisible force stops the "jiggle" from eventually pushing us back out into the desert forever?

Section 05Conclusion: Generation is Just Sampling

We have successfully proven that Generation is nothing but sampling. When you see a state-of-the-art AI model creating a photo of a cat, it is just a very sophisticated hiker dropped into a 1,000,000-dimensional desert, using a compass learned from millions of photos to take a guided walk through noise.

But even though we've demystified the "walk," the rabbit hole goes much deeper. If you're thinking from first principles, a few things should still feel like "magic":

  1. Why does the walk actually settle?

    You might wonder: if we are constantly adding noise (the "jiggle"), why don't the particles eventually just fly off into the desert forever?

    The answer lies in a beautiful physical Equilibrium. Think of a valley with a powerful fan blowing at the bottom. The "Drift" is gravity pulling you to the center; the "Noise" is the fan pushing you away. Langevin Dynamics is the mathematical proof that these two forces eventually cancel each other out in a way that the particles spend more time where the probability is high, perfectly tracing the "shape" of the data without ever flying away or collapsing to a single point.

  2. The Denoising Trick: How is "Noise" a "Gradient"?

    This was the biggest "Aha!" moment for me. In every Diffusion paper, the U-Net is trained to predict the noise that was added to an image. But we just said the U-Net is a Score Predictor (predicting gradients).

    How are they the same thing?

    Think about it: if you take a clean photo of a cat and push it into the desert with a bit of noise, the "Score" (the direction back to the cat) is exactly the opposite of the noise you just added. By teaching a model to guess what the noise was, you are implicitly teaching it the exact vector it needs to move in to get back to the "Cat Island." Predicting noise is predicting the gradient.

  3. The "Curse of Flatness" and the Forward Process

    If we want to generate cats, why do we start by destroying them with the "Forward Process"?

    In a 1,000,000-dimensional space, "Cat Island" is infinitesimally small. If you drop a hiker at random, they will almost never feel the slope of the island because the rest of the desert is perfectly flat.

    By adding noise to our training data (The Forward Process), we are effectively "blurring" the island, stretching its slope across the entire desert. We create a "gradient trail" that the hiker can feel from miles away. We break the data to make the path back to it visible.
