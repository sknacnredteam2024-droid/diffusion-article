Diffusion Models
from First Principles

Is it just a "Drunkard's Walk" to a cat? Understanding generative AI through Langevin sampling, mathematical physics, and a very lost hiker in the desert.

Deep Dive · ~18 min read · Generative Modelling · Mathematics

If you have spent any time looking into modern AI, you have seen the standard explanation for Diffusion Models:

The Forward Process: You take an image and slowly add Gaussian noise until it's just a cloud of static. The Reverse Process: You teach a neural network to "remove" that noise step-by-step to recover the image.

It sounds simple. Intuitive, even. But if you are like me, the "intuition" didn't quite click at first. It felt a bit like a magic trick — and it left me with a long list of Whys:

  • Why does adding noise work? If our goal is to create data, why do we start by destroying it?
  • What exactly is "noise removal"? Is the network really just a super-powered denoiser, or is something deeper happening?
  • Is the noise "structured"? Is that noise so special that its removal magically reveals a cat?
  • The biggest contradiction: Why do we add even more random Gaussian noise during the reverse process? If we are cleaning the image, why are we still throwing dirt on it?

In reality, that final step is governed by a beautiful piece of math. In actual Diffusion papers, you'll see a formula that looks like this:

$$x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t + (1 - \alpha_t) \nabla \log p_t(x_t) \right) + \color{#c8620a}{\sigma_t \mathbf{z}_t}$$
The amber term — a fresh burst of random noise added back at every single step

Note the amber term: that is a fresh burst of random Gaussian noise we add back in at every single step. It seems like we are sabotaging the cleanup, but as we will see, this "stochasticity" is the secret ingredient.

This series will not deal with specific neural network architectures (like U-Nets or Transformers) or niche implementation choices. Our goal is purely to understand the fundamental idea of Diffusion and Generative Modelling from the ground up. We start with Langevin Sampling.

Section 01What is Generation, Really?

If we strip away all the AI buzzwords, generation is nothing but sampling.

Whenever we talk about "generating" something — an image, a sentence, or a protein sequence — what we are actually doing is picking a single point out of a massive collection of possibilities. How we sample from a known distribution is the generation process itself.

The Simple Die (Discrete Sampling)

If I ask you to "generate" a random number between 1 and 6, you reach for a die. When you roll it and it lands on 4, you have successfully "generated" data. We are working with discrete variables — a Probability Mass Function (PMF):

$$P(X=k) = \frac{1}{6} \quad \text{for } k = 1, 2, \ldots, 6$$
Figure 2 — Discrete Sampling: Probability Mass Function

The Image Space (The 1,000,000-Sided Die)

Now map this back to images. A 1000×1000 pixel grayscale image lives in a 1,000,000-dimensional space — one point per image. To "generate" a new cat, we are essentially trying to roll a 1,000,000-dimensional die.

In this unfathomably large space, almost every "roll" results in noise. But somewhere in that desert, there are tiny "islands" of high probability — specific configurations of those million pixels that represent a cat.

Deep Generative Modeling is nothing more than finding a way to sample from that high-dimensional probability distribution. The catch? In reality, this distribution is unknown. We have cat photos, but not the mathematical equation for "cat-ness."
Figure 3 — The High-Dimensional Desert: Probability Landscape

Section 02The Wonderful Realization: Sculpting with Noise

Here is the "Aha!" moment. What if I told you we can sample from any complex distribution using only two ingredients?

  • Gaussian Noise — a simple cloud of random static (The Engine)
  • A Magic Compass — something technical people call the Score Function (The Guide)

This is the essence of Langevin Sampling.

The Gradient: Finding the "North" of Data

Imagine we actually had the secret recipe — the analytical formula $P(x)$ — for our target distribution. From that formula we can compute its Gradient $\nabla P(x)$: a vector pointing in the direction of steepest ascent. Like standing on a mountain in fog — the gradient tells you not just which way to go, but how steep the slope is, hinting at the step size needed to reach the peak.

The "Log" Twist: The Score Function

In practice, we don't use $\nabla P(x)$. We use the gradient of its logarithm:

$$\text{Score Function} \;=\; \nabla \log p(x)$$
The compass that points toward high-probability regions

Why the log? In high dimensions, the raw probability $P(x)$ of being in a cat region is so infinitesimally small that $\nabla P(x)$ becomes effectively zero — the slope is too flat to feel. By taking the logarithm, we mathematically "stretch" the landscape, turning flat plains into steep, feelable slopes.

For the Mathematically Inclined: The Proof

From the chain rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx} \log p(x) \;=\; \frac{p'(x)}{p(x)}$$

Suppose $p(x) = e^{-x^2}$ (a Gaussian). Compare the raw gradient vs. the score at $x = 10$:

Raw Gradient ∇p(x)
$$p'(x) = -2x\, e^{-x^2}$$
At x=10 → p(10) ≈ 3.7×10⁻⁴⁴ · · · vanishes to zero. Compass is dead.
Score Function ∇ log p(x)
$$\frac{d}{dx}\log p(x) = -2x$$
At x=10 → score = −20. Clear, usable signal!

The division by $p(x)$ cancelled the exponential decay that was killing our signal. The score is a rescaled gradient that remains informative even far from the data.

But How Do We Find This Score Function?

You might object: "You just derived this using P(x), but you said P(x) is unknown!"

Correct. This is the "million-dollar problem." In the real world, this is exactly what the Neural Network learns. When you see a U-Net "predicting noise," it is actually predicting the gradient of the log probability. These gradients look like noise when visualized in high dimensions, but they are precise instructions for our walk.

Section 03The Langevin Walk: A Helicopter Drop into Noise

Now we have our ingredients. Let's build the image intuitively.

Imagine you are dropped from a helicopter at a random point $x_0$ in a 1,000,000-dimensional desert. You see nothing but static. But you have your trusty Score Function Compass.

The Step

You check the compass at $x_0$. It tells you how much and in which direction to move:

$$x_1 = x_0 + \eta \,\nabla \log p(x_0)$$
η (eta) = step size. How much we trust the compass.

The Optimization Trap

If you only followed the compass, everyone dropped from the helicopter would converge to the exact same "perfect average cat." We would lose all variety — generating a single blurry mean rather than diverse samples.

The "Jiggle": Introducing Randomness

To see the whole island, you need to dance as you walk. Every time you take a step toward the peak, you take a tiny random hop in a random direction. This gives us the full Langevin Equation:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \underbrace{\eta \,\nabla \log p(x_t)}_{\text{Drift (Compass)}} + \underbrace{\color{#c8620a}{\sqrt{2\eta}\;\mathbf{z}_t}}_{\text{Diffusion (Jiggle)}}$$
The amber term is what makes generation diverse. Without it, every sample is identical.

Why $\sqrt{2\eta}$ specifically? Three reasons:

  • The √η scaling: In Brownian motion, random kicks grow with the square root of time, keeping the noise-to-drift ratio consistent across different step sizes.
  • The factor of 2: This is the mathematical "sweet spot" that ensures the walk settles into the exact shape of p(x) — not a wider or narrower version.
  • Why Gaussian? The Central Limit Theorem: the sum of millions of tiny independent kicks always converges to a Gaussian. It's the only jiggle that allows perfectly smooth exploration.
Figure 4 — The Langevin Walk: Drift + Diffusion

Section 04The Grand Connection: Mapping Langevin Back to Diffusion

Look at the "scary" formula from the introduction side-by-side with our Langevin walk:

Diffusion Reverse Step (from papers)
$$x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \!\left( x_t + (1-\alpha_t)\nabla \log p_t(x_t) \right) + \color{#c8620a}{\sigma_t \mathbf{z}_t}$$
Our Langevin Walk
$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \eta\,\nabla \log p(x_t) + \color{#c8620a}{\sqrt{2\eta}\,\mathbf{z}_t}$$
The math is the same. The coefficients are just "volume knobs" — $\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}$ and $(1-\alpha_t)$ are engineered so the noise schedule anneals correctly. We have proven: Generation is nothing but sampling.

Section 05Conclusion: But the Rabbit Hole Goes Deeper

When you see a state-of-the-art AI model creating a photo of a cat, it is just a very sophisticated hiker dropped into a 1,000,000-dimensional desert, using a compass learned from millions of photos to take a guided walk through noise.

But even though we've demystified the "walk," a few things should still feel like magic:

  1. Why does the walk actually settle?
    If we're constantly adding noise, why don't particles fly off forever? The answer is Equilibrium. Think of a valley with a fan at the bottom: drift is gravity pulling to the center, diffusion is the fan pushing away. Langevin Dynamics proves these forces cancel in exactly the right way — particles spend more time where probability is high, without flying away or collapsing to a single point.
  2. The Denoising Trick: How is "Noise" a "Gradient"?
    If you take a clean cat photo and add noise, the Score (direction back to the cat) is exactly the opposite of the noise you added. Teaching a model to predict the noise implicitly teaches it the gradient. Predicting noise is predicting the gradient.
  3. The "Curse of Flatness" and the Forward Process
    In 1,000,000 dimensions, "Cat Island" is infinitesimally small — a hiker will almost never feel its slope. By adding noise to training data (the Forward Process), we blur the island, stretching its slope across the entire desert. We break the data to make the path back to it visible.
